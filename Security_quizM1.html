Ransomware is a type of malicious software designed to block access to a computer system or data until a sum of money is paid. It is one of the most common and dangerous forms of cyberattacks today.

During my time as an IT intern at Pridgeon and Clay, a manufacturer of metal components for the automotive industry, I learned about a ransomware attack that occurred the year before I joined. The attackers used a brute force method to guess the password to the company's servers, gaining unauthorized access.

The ransomware encrypted critical company files, making them inaccessible. This attack disrupted operations and highlighted the importance of robust cybersecurity measures. Fortunately, the company did not pay the ransom. Instead, they rebuilt their systems from scratch, which was a costly but necessary step to ensure security.

Since the attack, the company has implemented stronger security practices, such as hardening server defenses, educating employees about phishing and email scams, and enforcing strict policies like locking computers when unattended.

This experience underscores the importance of proactive measures to prevent ransomware attacks. Organizations should regularly update software, use strong passwords, enable multi-factor authentication, and maintain secure backups to recover data without paying ransoms.

Ransomware attacks can happen to anyone, and being prepared with a solid cybersecurity strategy is the best defense against such threats.

Here are different types of attacks that can happen to anyone online:

Ransomeware (Malicious software designed to block access to a computer system or data until a sum of money is paid).

Trojan (Cyber Threat in which malicious software pretends to be legitimate, harmless software to trick users into installing or executing it).

Worm (Malicious software that self-replicates itself like a worm without the need of humans).

Keylogger (Malicious Software records every keystroke you type on your keyboard).

Rootkit (Gives unauthorized/privileged access to a computer while concealing its presence).

Logic Bomb (Malicious code that remains hidden until a condition triggers it like a bomb).

Bloatware (Unwanted preinstalled, or bundled software is used to steal personal data).

Virus (Malicious software embeds itself into a system, replicating by modifying other programs/executing harmful actions).

SpyWare (Secretly infects your machine to monitor your activity, this includes your screen/passwords everything digital).
